  • Is our education system heading in the right direction?
    Posted Date February 4, 2016

    One of the many things that come to our mind when we speak about out childhood is education. Taking this thought a step deeper, one may realise that a major differentiating factor between a successful educated child and a not so successful* child is; the quality of education that a child was ‘privy’ to. I write this word ‘privy’ with a conscious effort to highlight that in a country like ours which is emerging and running against time to compete with the developed economies, completion of minimum school education has become more like a tick in a box scenario. There is a constant effort to reach a particular literacy level keeping other emerging countries as a benchmark. However, a missing link could be whether, as a whole are there steps taken to elevate our society and our country by improving the existing educational infrastructure.

    A very recent study supporting the view mentioned in paragraph above was published in Times of India, which read “Most Std. X students below par in English, Maths: NCERT1”

    Further an extract of NCERT Vision 2020 read as below:

    “At present India has achieved a literacy rate of 64% but still the gross number of illiterates are the largest in the world. India has the third largest scientific and technological manpower of the world but it has also a high drop-out rate, only 2 out of 10 students reach the 9th standards. India has institutions like Indian Institute of Technology, Indian Institute of Management and several schools that can be compared with the best of the institutions in the world. It also has institutions where required infrastructure facilities are awfully lacking. There is a great disparity of educational achievements between good public schools and government run institutions in rural and slum metropolitan areas.2”

    There is a realisation of the fact that there is a massive scope of improvement to spearhead efforts to achieve a 100% literacy rate in our country. However, equally significant is the lateral qualitative growth of education which encompasses students from different strata of economic society.

    Although the journey to achieve this is long enough, there is an optimistic vision that has been factored by the planning commission. The 12th Five Year Plan (2012-2017) of government has identified four major priorities with respect to education viz. access, equity, quality and governance3. There is still some time to assess the progress of various schemes launched by government to attain these objectives. For the time being our country can be hopeful that the seeds have been sown which will bear the fruits of a holistic educational growth.

    Note: *(not considering only the earnings potential but also factoring the social learnings)
