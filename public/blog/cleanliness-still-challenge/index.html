  • Cleanliness: Why is it still a challenge?
    Posted Date December 21, 2016

    Cleanliness is next to godliness. It is said so, as by being clean we help not only ourselves but others as well. Cleanliness is one such noble act that can be practiced easily and conveniently by everyone for a clean and healthy environment. It does not only refer to personal hygiene but also encompasses every aspect of ensuring public hygiene.

    India has been blessed in many ways. We are one of the oldest civilizations on the planet however; one thing our great nation is still deficit in is a sense of cleanliness.

    So why is cleanliness an issue despite it being a common civic sense?

    A large portion of our nation still does not realize that a myriad of diseases can be prevented by the simple act of cleanliness. Diseases like dengue fever, swine flu, malaria, chicken pox and jaundice can be possibly avoided before having to search for a cure if ensured a clean environment.

    The Indian ignorance and lack of awareness can be easily seen in the daily household scenario. Many Indian houses are meticulously cleaned and spotless. Yet right outside rows of these clean houses, there are filthy streets with mountains of garbage. This shows that the public sphere is a concept in Indian society that has traditionally been absent.

    We are the same Indians who when traveling/living outside India would take utmost care not to drop even a toffee wrapper on the roadside. We are the same Indians who would blindly follow every rule in other countries but while in our own country we do not think twice before following common social etiquettes. We are the very ones who love to keep our own house and surroundings clean but wouldn’t mind dumping our wastes into our neighbor’s compound. This is the irony of Indians.

    But the blame cannot be entirely put on the general public. The lack of amenities like public toilets and waste bins is also partly responsible for all these misdeeds. But it still needs to be seen how much Indians would adhere to these new systems and take a bit of extra pain and trouble to drop the waste wrapper in the dustbin. Despite such facilities provided in some places we can often find people who prefer to relieve themselves in the open than pay a rupee for the use-and-pay toilets. Most of the bins on the roadsides are full and spill over due to either lack of regular disposal by the civic authorities or due to the general trend of the public to throw waste items around the bin than in it. Hence awareness among the authorities is necessary so that stricter regulations and greater amenities can be implemented for public well-being.

    Tourism is another such aspect that is hit by the unclean habits of the Indian community. Our surroundings and our way of life is the impression foreign tourists carry of India, which sadly is not that of a clean country. The aesthetic beauty of several popular tourist places is ruined due to this lack of awareness and ignorance or apathy.

    What is needed in India is a Japanese-style attitude towards cleanliness where everyone, no matter their social status, learns that it is his or her duty to clean shared spaces, starting in elementary school. For this, first and foremost, an ideology has to be well phrased and publicized throughout the country. Our present Prime Minister, Mr. Modi has struck the right note when he launched the national Swachh Bharat campaign at the start of his office.

    We Indians need to change our mentality and cultivate a more civic approach. We need to go beyond personal cleanliness to collective cleanliness, which can only be achieved by realizing the fact that cleanliness is an effortless way of living that ultimately leads to healthy lifestyle.

    – Akanksha Agarwal
