  • The Menace of Open Defecation
    Posted Date December 27, 2016

    ‘Open defecation’ means the practice of people defecating in open or in other words not into a designated toilet. Open defecation causes public health problems in areas where people defecate in fields, urban parks, rivers, and open trenches in close proximity to the living space of others.
    About one billion people, or 15 percent of the global population, practice open defecation. India has the highest number of people practicing open defecation, around 490 million people, i.e. nearly a third of the population. Most of it occurs in rural areas as opposed to urban areas. It means for every 3 residents one defecates in the open, or to put it in a different way, a member of each family is forced into such practice. That is pure Discrimination!
    High levels of open defecation in a country are usually correlated with high rate of infant mortality and health issues.

    Reasons for prevalence of open defecation:
    There can be many reasons why a person is forced to defecate in the open, and these may include:
    •No toilets are available at households or workplaces (during farming activities or at school).
    •A toilet is available but is of “low quality”, such as: toilets are filthy, dark, foul smelling, dilapidated or unattractive (often this is the case for shared or public toilets).
    •Toilets are only at some distance and it may be dangerous to get there at night.
    •Ignorance about the benefits of using toilets.
    •Open defecation is embedded as a routine or social norm or there are social taboos (father-in-law not using the same toilet as daughter-in-law)

    Health impacts
    Open defecation is an important factor in causing various diseases, most notably diarrhea and intestinal worm infections but also typhoid, cholera, hepatitis, polio, trachoma and others. Open defecation can lead to water pollution, and so waterborne diseases, when rain flushes feces that are dispersed in the environment into surface water or unprotected wells.

    Safety and gender impacts
    There are also strong gender impacts: the lack of safe, private toilets makes women and girls vulnerable to violence and is an impediment to girls’ education. Rapes and sexual molestation take place when women search for places for open defecation that are secluded and private, often during hours of darkness.

    Eradication of this practice must be the utmost priority of Health Organisations and there is a need of more initiatives like the ‘Swachch Bharat Abhiyan’. But there are other vital factors too that are key drivers for the elimination of open defecation. Without political will and support it’s a humongous task to bring about such a change at national level because the revolution is based on changing the behaviour and mindset of the masses.

    Technology also plays an essential role in advancement of society and brings about revolutionary changes that is a need of the hour. There are some simple sanitation technology options available to reduce open defecation prevalence –

    Google Maps Toilet Locator: The ‘toiler locator’ feature has been added to Google Maps in collaboration with the Ministry of Urban Development (MoUD). To use the new feature, Google Maps users on Android, iOS, and desktop will need to search for ‘public toilets’. They will then be shown public toilets near them (currently in Delhi/NCR and Bhopal and Indore), alongside the exact address and opening hours. The feature is available in both English and Hindi. Each public toilet listing when opened also features a star rating, helping determine whether the toilet is well reviewed by previous visitors.

    Toilet bags: A more advanced solution of the plastic toilet bag has been provided by the Swedish company Peepoople who are producing the “Peepoo bag”, a “personal, single-use, self-sanitising, fully biodegradable toilet that prevents feces from contaminating the immediate area as well as the surrounding ecosystem”.

    Bucket toilets and urine diversion: Bucket toilets are a simple portable toilet option. They can be upgraded in various ways, one of them being urine diversion which can make them similar to urine-diverting dry toilets. Urine diversion can significantly reduce odours from dry toilets.

    Eliminating open defecation is the main aim of improving access to sanitation worldwide and is a proposed indicator for sustainable development goals. Even if toilets are available, people still need to be convinced to refrain from open defecation and use toilets. Therefore, the need for behavioural changes is critical in addition to the provision of toilets.
