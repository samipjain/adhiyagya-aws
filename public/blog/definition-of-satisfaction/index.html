  • The definition of "Satisfaction"
    Posted Date April 13, 2018

    The word ‘Satisfaction’ has different meanings in the dictionary for different people. Judging by the commonality of human nature most of us have financial and job stability as an answer to this question. To some people (very less percentage of this world) ‘Satisfaction’ has different meaning altogether; it means mental happiness and inner peace. This mental satisfaction can range from “pursuing your hobby as your job” or “mountain climbing” or “helping out underprivileged children with their studies”.

    It so happened that I bumped into my mental satisfaction a few months back when I joined Adhiyagya. I belong to the Mumbai volunteer team. We go to a school in Mankhurd, Mumbai to teach English. The class roughly consists of around 30 children and each child has a different story to tell. Our class basically starts with the revision of the topic of the previous class transitioning to the next topic.

    Our class needs at least two volunteers because each child requires personal attention even during the class as well. There is a lot of patience involved form both the parties. Our patience comprises of our capacity to repetitively answer the same questions with the same enthusiasm whereas their patience comprises of us trying to figure out their minds and their capacity to understand things.

    There have been various instances during our classes where we see an example of “BETTER UNDERSTANDING THROUGH SIMPLIFICATION”. We as volunteers try to simplify things with examples that are real and understandable. Hence a little complex English topic becomes less overwhelming for them. A child always cherishes his/her comfort zone (We all do!). These children find English a little overwhelming and out of their comfort zone so we as teachers face a challenge where we have to drag a child out of his comfort zone and make him realize that English is not that bad after all.

    Our greatest driving factor is that the students want to learn and they don’t go by the clock. During our childhood our adults often told us that we were lucky to have gotten the best schooling and education one could get. One rarely understands this fact until one sees these children. With bare minimum facilities most these children come to school without a single complain because they have a knack for learning and growing. This serves as an encouragement for us to get up on a weekend morning and go to teach these children.

    Talking about inspiration there are many factors that keep us going. Compliments like “Aap logo ka padhaya bahut ache se samajh mein aata hai” and naggings like “Abhi 15 mins aur padhao” give us a euphoric punch of energy every time. These lines though very basic are the biggest compliments a teacher could get. Though it sounds very dramatic, but in these moments I feel like my chaotic life starts making some sense.

    Teaching those children has a nostalgic touch to it as well. We relive our school life in little snippets. Sitting only beside our best friend and turning our heads reflexively to talk to our friend seated right behind us are some of the things we all have done in our times as well. Our teacher’s scolding starts making all the sense now.

    My experience as a volunteer has been amazing so far. These children have taught me many things in exchange. They have taught me how little my struggles are and how miniature my problems have been. The joy of living is in giving and how euphoric our inner peace can be are some of the lessons my experience has bestowed upon me.

    – Medha Gupta (Volunteer, Adhiyagya – Mumbai Centre)
